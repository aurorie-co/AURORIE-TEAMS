# Customer Comms Skill

Use when drafting any customer-facing communication: ticket responses, holding messages,
escalation notices, or bulk notification templates.

## When to Use
- Any customer-facing response in support workflows
- Escalation holding messages
- Bulk or template responses for recurring issues

## Core Principles

### 1. Acknowledge Before Solving
Name the specific problem the customer experienced before jumping to a solution.

**Do not**: "Thank you for contacting support. We apologize for the inconvenience."
**Do**: "It sounds like your export failed mid-way and you lost progress — that's frustrating and I want to get this resolved."

### 2. Be Direct About What Happens Next
Customers in distress do not want vague assurances. Tell them exactly what will happen and when.

**Do not**: "We will look into this and get back to you as soon as possible."
**Do**: "I'm escalating this to our engineering team now. You'll receive an update within 4 hours, by 5 PM EST."

### 3. Match Urgency to Priority
| Priority | Tone | Response speed target |
|----------|------|-----------------------|
| P0 | Urgent, direct, warm | Immediate holding response within 30 min |
| P1 | Helpful, focused | Within 2 hours |
| P2 | Friendly, clear | Within 1 business day |
| P3 | Informative, educational | Within 2 business days |

### 4. Never Over-Apologize
One genuine acknowledgment of the problem is sufficient. Repeated apologies read as filler and reduce trust.

### 5. One Action Item to Close
End every response with exactly one clear action — either something the customer should do,
or something your team will do (with a timeframe).

## Tone Calibration

### For Bug Reports / Outages
- Acknowledge the impact specifically
- Take ownership ("we")
- Give a timeline for resolution or update
- Avoid technical jargon

### For How-To / Feature Questions
- Be instructional and patient
- Use numbered steps
- Link to documentation if available
- Invite follow-up

### For Billing / Refund Issues
- Be empathetic but do not commit to refunds you cannot authorize
- Describe the process clearly
- Set expectations for how long it takes

### For Security or Data Concerns
- Take the concern seriously, even if likely unfounded
- Do not reassure prematurely — investigate first
- Involve legal/security before responding if data may have been exposed

## Structure Template

```
[Specific acknowledgment of the customer's experience]

[Answer or next step — direct and concrete]

[Timeframe or what happens next]

[Single closing action item]

[Sign-off with name and role]
```

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- "As per my previous email..." — condescending
- "Unfortunately..." as an opener — telegraphs bad news badly
- Blaming the customer for causing the issue — never, even if true
- Promising a fix by a date engineering hasn't committed to
- Forwarding internal tickets or jira links to customers
